üöó The Ford Edsel ‚Äî Problems Are Markets

$350 million ($3.5 billion adjusted) ‚Äî the most famous marketing failure in history.

Ford had a gap in the middle of their product line. They spent a fortune launching the Edsel to fill it. Nobody bought it. Why? Because automobile buyers weren't waking up worried about the middle of Ford's product line. The "problem" Ford solved was its own, not the customer's.

Bernard Baruch's 12-Word Secret

"Find out what people want, then show them how to get it."
‚Äî Bernard Baruch, adviser to six presidents

Baruch could walk into a room full of egos so large they had trouble fitting through the front door ‚Äî Wall Street titans, presidents, senators ‚Äî all with different points of view, determined to get their way. Twenty minutes later, everyone would walk out saying, "Bernard Baruch has the solution, and we are all in agreement." His method: sincerely probe each person to find out what they really want, then work together to find a mutual solution.

"No Problem, No Sale"

A master salesman's simple advice: no problem, no sale. I take an aspirin only when I have a headache. You can be the most brilliant copywriter in the world, but you won't convince me to take an aspirin if I don't have a headache. Problems are markets. The bigger, more extensive, and more painful the problem, the bigger your opportunity.

Demographics are not markets. I never bought an aspirin because I'm a member of a demographic group. I take an aspirin when I have a headache, not because I want a relationship with my druggist!

‚öñÔ∏è The Apothecary Scale

Every time you sell something, picture an apothecary scale. Price on the left. Benefits + belief on the right.

As desire goes up, price resistance goes down. The fishermen with their $15,000 bass boats and $30,000 SUVs won't begrudge you $5 for worms. Maybe double to $10. Or forget the worms entirely ‚Äî sell the secret map of the lake's best fishing holes for $100.

A great salesman once said: when a qualified prospect says "it's too expensive," what they're really saying is you haven't given me enough believable benefit yet.

üîí The 12 Power Proof Elements

Session 4 ‚Äî The Secret of Master Closers

Anyone can make a promise, but nobody buys without belief. Proof is the rarest element in advertising ‚Äî and that's exactly why the brain trusts it most. The strongest ads pile layer upon layer of proof.

"Without belief, nobody buys. The higher the belief, the higher the response. Make compelling proof the alpha and omega of your advertising."
‚Äî Gary Bencivenga
Proof #1
Demonstration

Hopkins: "No argument in the world can ever compare with one dramatic demonstration."

Otis Elevator (1855): At the Crystal Palace Exposition, Otis stood on an open-sided elevator, had his assistant cut the rope cables with an axe while thousands gaped below. The crowd gasped ‚Äî surely he'd plunge to his death. The elevator dropped a few inches and stopped. This one demonstration changed world architecture forever ‚Äî cities could finally build higher than 4 stories. Before this, nobody would rent upper floors.

Ron Popeil: Not an eloquent writer, but a brilliant salesman making fortunes by demonstrating products on late-night TV ‚Äî slicers, dicers, chicken roasters. The demonstration IS the selling.

"Do You Make These Mistakes in English?" ‚Äî control for 40 YEARS! Right in the first paragraph, it rattles off grammatical gaffes that many people make. If you read them and realize you commit them yourself, you're already half convinced you need the course.
Timex "Takes a Licking and Keeps on Ticking" ‚Äî strapped to the propeller of a motorboat, strapped to turtles photographed underwater. Went from unknown to America's largest-selling watch ‚Äî still is today.
Proof #2
Creative Guarantee

A bold, specific guarantee that becomes the selling point itself. Don't just guarantee ‚Äî make the guarantee do the selling.

Domino's: "Fresh, hot pizza in 30 minutes or less, guaranteed." Tom Monaghan went from college student to one of the world's richest men. Captured 25-30% market share in 50 major metro areas ‚Äî all from one proof element. Nothing about Mama's sauce recipe. Nothing about extra-crispy crust. Just: you want pizza fast, we guarantee it fast.
Rodale's return label: Printed a return postage label right in the mailing. Recipients could see it before ordering ‚Äî instant proof the guarantee is real. Raised response across the board.
"21 incredible days of the most erotic, thrilling sex of your life ‚Äî or you pay nothing." The guarantee IS the selling. Instead of standard guarantee language, the guarantee copy enflames desire while removing risk. Ran for years as a blockbuster.
More variants: 110% Money-Back Guarantee ‚Ä¢ Multiple Benefit Guarantee (review all benefits IN the guarantee) ‚Ä¢ "Try Us Free" (valid even with cash up front if you offer money-back)
Proof #3
Reason Why

Ogilvy, when asked about reason-why advertising: "Is there any other kind?"

Slice: "A better tasting soft drink because it contains 10% fruit juice." In a notoriously difficult market, those seven words captured 7% of a $30 billion industry. Remove the "because" phrase and you have a pure claim that rolls off your back. Add it, and curiosity ignites. Seven words = a $2 billion business.
Kleenex: "Absorbs 50% more" (bare claim ‚Äî no traction) vs. explaining it has two plies = instant acceptance. When people understand the mechanism behind the claim, they can believe it.
"Speak Spanish like a diplomat" ‚Äî the language course was created by the U.S. State Department to teach newly appointed diplomats. That's the entire proof element. You can use almost any words you want; what closes the sale is this amazingly persuasive reason why.
"Half Off Sale" ‚Üí makes little impression, probably a gimmick. "Fire Sale! 50% off because of our recent fire" ‚Üí now you're intrigued. The reason why makes the discount believable.
Proof #4
Specifics

Specific numbers and details signal research and truth. Vague = suspicious.

The cruise headline (most successful ever, 25 words): "Take only 110 days, spend just $11,033 for this 26,000-mile world cruise to 21 fascinating ports in 14 famed countries. Shore excursions abroad included." If anyone tells you long headlines don't pull ‚Äî tell them to go take a cruise.
"62 of the most significant yet under-publicized medical discoveries" ‚Äî the word "under-publicized" hit a raw nerve. Everyone knows medical breakthroughs exist that the media ignores. Outpulled the control by 250%.
"Free Gift for Mothers Only" ‚Äî that very targeted, specific headline was the control for about 30 years! A colossal, long-running control that sold a ton of subscriptions.
Magalog evolution insight: LESS copy on covers can mean MORE sales ‚Äî because it looks more like a real magazine. One big idea is often worth more than a string of multiple benefits. The more bullets you cram on, the more it flags itself as advertising.
Proof #5
The Expos√©

Become the consumer's champion. Call out the bad actors and change the battlefield.

"The Great Vitamin Hoax" ‚Äî Dr. Julian Whitaker, a famous champion of vitamins, attacks vitamins. The market couldn't ignore it: "Wait, you're the guy telling me to take handfuls of vitamins, and now it's a hoax?" Payoff: too many charlatans cutting corners. Subscribe to learn the best brands. When competitors copied him, it changed the entire battlefield.
"Surprise" is pure catnip in headlines. Gary used it with every presidential election cycle ‚Äî "Election Surprise!" ‚Äî and it always worked. Phillips would trot it out every 4 years.
Mark Skousen's "Lies Politicians Tell": Positioned as the white knight taking on scoundrels ‚Äî lying brokers, lying politicians, Darth Vader IRS agents. Featured hysterically funny caricatures. One of the biggest winners in financial publishing history.
Gary Halbert's "sworn under oath" technique: Gary adapted this ‚Äî instead of having the editor swear under oath (which might imply investigation), he had the editor "admit" his CIA background and "deny" giving illegal inside information. Reverse psychology as consumer champion.
Proof #6
Explain the Mechanism

Show HOW it works, not just that it works. When readers understand the way you do something, they can believe the benefits you claim.

Richard Band's Profitable Investing: The mailing explains Richard's precise screening methodology ‚Äî how he systematically filters out risky and overpriced stocks and focuses on screaming bargains, just like Warren Buffett. When readers understand the mechanism, the promise of wealth becomes believable.
Rosser Reeves' cigarette ad: A masterpiece of mechanism + authority. Announced a Reader's Digest article confirming the claims, introduced "filtered cigarette smoke" as the mechanism, included an easy-to-understand illustration, and featured a doctor explaining the importance. 130 words of copy. Monthly sales jumped from 50 million to 60M, then 100M, 150M, 200M and up. All from explaining the mechanism.
Proof #7
Sell Against Type

Make a case AGAINST something your market loves. Triggers massive, irresistible curiosity.

"The Case Against Tax Exempts" beat a gold stocks headline by MULTIPLE HUNDREDS of percent. Everyone thought it would bomb ‚Äî it sounded boring. But: the bond market is 9x larger than the stock market, and investors had their life savings in tax exempts. They HAD to find out what was wrong.
The "Case Against..." formula: Fill in the blank with something your prospects are already committed to = guaranteed readership. "The Case Against Attending Gary Bencivenga's Seminar" ‚Äî if you already paid, could you ignore that headline?
John Dessauer "The Case AGAINST International Mutual Funds" ‚Äî from the man who championed them for years. A shocker that made legions of investors wonder: "Has he done a 180 on us?"
Conservative Richard Band says "I'm thrilled about Clinton's reelection" ‚Äî a beloved conservative financial advisor, cherished by wealthy Republican investors, celebrating a Democrat. Coming from a partisan it would be nothing. Coming against type, it was startling.
Proof #8
Acknowledge Disbelief

Name the skepticism before they feel it. You did it for them ‚Äî so they don't have to.

"Believe it or not..." ‚Äî an "umbrella-style" headline that lets you fit lots of unrelated benefits under one headline. "Believe it or not, I've discovered an investment that's safer than your bank and offers you all these advantages..." Then just list everything. Very easy to write, extremely effective.
Gene Schwartz: "Should any man be given so much sexual power over women?" ‚Äî phrasing an outlandish claim as a question. Provocative, amusing ‚Äî and deadly effective.
When the writer brings skepticism to the foreground, it saves the reader from being skeptical. The reader thinks: "I would normally find that hard to believe, but the speaker acknowledges it too. Maybe I can trust this person."
Proof #9
Highly Believable Source

Borrow credibility from a trusted authority. The more credible the source, the more credible the message.

Warren Buffett: "I advise you to subscribe. I read each issue religiously. Anyone interested in investing who doesn't subscribe is making a big mistake." ‚Äî possibly the most powerful endorsement in the history of newsletter advertising. The most successful investor who ever walked the earth, saying YOU'RE MAKING A MISTAKE if you don't subscribe.
Gary saw this ad and immediately called Phillips Publishing, urging them to BUY the newsletter ‚Äî just to get this testimonial. But they wouldn't sell. The ad was poorly designed ‚Äî it buried the most powerful endorsement in history in a weak headline: "I'd advise you to subscribe." A vastly undervalued marketing asset.
Proof #10
Testimonials

Must sound REAL, not polished. Ogilvy called this a "burr of singularity" ‚Äî rough-edged, spontaneous, real. A copywriter's polished prose kills believability.

"Guys like me need guys like you and our wives to protect us" ‚Äî the "and our wives" is what makes it real. A copywriter would never have thought to add that. That part lets you know it came from a real guy, with a real wife who looks out for him.
"When I've cleared a million dollars, I'll be out to buy you a lunch" ‚Äî you suspect the person is real because the quote sounds so real. It gives you the feeling: "You just can't make this stuff up!"
If you're desperate for a testimonial but don't have one ‚Äî get one from the owner. Format it like a testimonial. It works better than you'd think.
Proof #11
Candor ‚Äî Gary's FAVORITE

Being less certain makes you MORE trusted. This is one you can have all to yourself because so few dare to be candid in their advertising.

"May rise" beat "will rise" ‚Äî investors know nobody can predict the future. When you pretend to, you lose credibility. The word "may" greatly outpulled "will." Counterintuitive ‚Äî but your prospects are smarter than you think.
"Fix almost any part of almost any car" beat the confident version ‚Äî Caples' test. Mechanics said: "Fix ANY part of ANY car? Come on!" The overclaim killed believability. Adding "almost" twice made it a hot seller.
"Get Rich Slowly" ‚Äî the client said "Are you serious, Gary?" In a world drowning in get-rich-quick hype, this was a monster success. The copy attacked the hype, sided with the prospect: "If you're looking for overnight riches, this isn't for you. But if you want a million dollars over a normal working lifetime..."
Shackleton's Antarctic ad: "Low wages, bitter cold, long months of complete darkness, constant danger, safe return doubtful. Honor and recognition in case of success." Half of England wanted to go! By admitting every terrible thing first, the two benefits at the end hit with MASSIVE force.
The Contrast Principle: Put your right hand in freezing water, left in hot. Then both in lukewarm. One hand feels it as cold, the other as hot ‚Äî same water! Shackleton's structure: shortcoming...shortcoming...shortcoming...BENEFIT ‚Äî makes the benefit feel enormous by contrast.
Ogilvy: "The antique dealer who points out a scratch always wins my trust."
Callas, Powell's cocky guarantee ad: "We'll guarantee to outpull your best ad by at least 10%... we'll even refund half your media expense." They held their breaths and ran it. The result? NOT ONE client cared about the test. Every single one said: "Forget the test. Running an offer like that, you sound like you know what you're doing. Let's get started right away."
Proof #12
Specialization

The more you specialize, the more people seek you out. You don't have to sell ‚Äî they come to you.

"If you have a brain tumor, you don't go to your family doctor." You want a neurosurgeon. And if the tumor is on the left side with some arcane condition, you want the specialist WITHIN the neurosurgeon community. If he specializes in exactly what you've got, the sale is almost closed before you even meet.
Department stores are dying because they try to be everything to everybody in an age of specialization. A formula for disaster ‚Äî another chain goes bankrupt every month.
The Pink Store: A store in NYC's Time Warner Center that sells exclusively pink accessories. That's how specialized stores have become.
Sneakers ‚Üí running shoes ‚Üí trail runners: When I was a kid, there was one type of sneaker (PF Flyers). Today: tennis shoes, basketball shoes, running shoes, hiking shoes, walking shoes. As soon as you learn there are "runners' shoes," can anything else be good enough? That's how powerful specialization is. It sells you instantly.
Gary created 11 different letterheads ‚Äî each featuring a different specialization. Financial newsletters. Health newsletters. Book clubs. When a potential client asked for info, he'd send it on the appropriate letterhead. "I usually got the job."

10. Romance
Travel, stimulation, excitement, seduction. The thrill of something new.

üìê The 5 Laws of Desire

Law #1
Infinite and Never-Ending

Lord Keynes worried prosperity would kill motivation. He was wrong. We work harder than ever. Desire never stops.

Law #2
Replaced Almost as Soon as Fulfilled

Wanted something badly, got it, wanted something else within a week. The cycle never ends.

Law #3
Hard to Create, Easy to Intensify

Don't sell cars to people not in the market. Sell to those already hot to buy. You can't manufacture desire ‚Äî only amplify existing ones.

Law #4
Quantity ‚Üí Quality

Off-the-rack suit ‚Üí Brioni suit. Hershey's Kisses ‚Üí Godiva ‚Üí Richart chocolates. "There is almost always a higher-priced version your customers will gobble up."

Law #5
General ‚Üí Specialized

IBM mainframes ‚Üí desktops ‚Üí laptops ‚Üí handhelds ‚Üí thousands of specialized computers. Sneakers ‚Üí running shoes ‚Üí marathon shoes. "Always ask: how is demand for my product most likely to subdivide next?"

Fear of loss >> desire for gain ‚Äî "Your snow tires are being stolen" gets you out of bed at 2 AM. "Free snow tires" doesn't.
